# The Grand Compromise — Education in the Age of AI

A 25-minute presentation. Audience: university students, the dean, software engineers.
Structure: four topics on a left-to-right journey canvas, framed by a live AI demo.

Core thesis: The goals of education were never wrong. The methods were a compromise
forced by scarcity. AI removes the scarcity, so the methods — not the goals — must change.

## Topic 1: The Compromises

### Why are you sitting like this?
Same room. Same hour. Same pace. Thirty people, one voice.
The answer isn't pedagogy. It's economics.
335 BC — Aristotle tutors Alexander at Mieza: a personal philosopher for a prince
and a handful of companions. The ideal always existed. Almost no one could afford it.

### Compromise I — The Lecture (scarce books)
Lectura — "a reading." Books were hand-copied and precious, so one master read the
text aloud and thirty wrote it down.
One book — months of a scribe's labour.
Gutenberg, ~1450 — the constraint dies.
The format has now outlived the printing press by five centuries.

### Compromise II — Batch Processing (scarce teachers)
Myth: "Schools were designed to produce factory workers." Historians call that story
invented — Prussia built its system before its factories, for literacy, loyalty,
citizenship. The real constraint was simpler:
One trained teacher. Thirty children. One pace for everyone.
Age cohorts, fixed curriculum, bells. Horace Mann imported the chassis in 1843.
We still drive it.

### Compromise III — The Exam Machine (scarce assessment)
You cannot examine every student, orally, for hours.
Imperial China's keju → Boston, 1845: written, standardized, batch-graded —
to compare many students at once.
A grade — lossy compression of a human.
A diploma — a trust signal for strangers.

### And it worked.
Mass literacy. Social mobility. The modern world.
The right trade — for its century.

## Topic 2: The Measurement and the Break

### The 2 Sigma Problem (1984)
Benjamin Bloom: students tutored 1-on-1 with mastery learning scored ~2 sigma above
the conventional classroom.
Honest footnote: the full 2 sigma never replicated — meta-analyses find 0.3–0.8 sigma.
Still among the largest effects ever measured in education.
Bloom's own verdict: tutoring is "too costly for most societies to bear on a large scale."

### What just became cheap
Patient explanation — in any style, at 2 a.m.
Instant feedback on every attempt.
A tutor that never runs out of time.
Plausible code. Plausible essays. Plausible everything.
The scarcity behind every compromise is evaporating.

### What breaks — precisely
Unsupervised assessment no longer proves authorship: homework, take-home essays,
semester projects.
"Plagiarism" assumed you copied from a human.
In-person, oral, practical assessment becomes more load-bearing — not less.
The degree signal: an open question, not a corpse.

### It's not autocomplete anymore
An architect's day with agents: decompose → delegate → review → verify.
The junior-dev-shaped hole in the org chart is a student-shaped hole in the curriculum.
Plausible code became cheap. Working, maintained, in-production code did not.
The gap is where judgment lives.

## Topic 4: The Undrawn Future

### What's scarce now
Judgment. Decomposition. Verification. Taste. The right question.
Bjork's desirable difficulties: what feels harder is what produces durable learning.
Effortless fluency is the feeling of not learning.
AI doesn't remove the need for effort. It makes skipping it frictionless.
Your six weeks weren't made worthless. What changed is which part was the point.

### Sketches, not solutions
Oral defense of AI-assisted work.
"Here's AI output — find the flaw."
Grade the process, not the artifact.
AI tutors set the pace; humans judge and motivate.
Every sketch re-spends scarce teacher time. That's the point: the cost collapse can
fund expensive assessment by cheapening instruction.

### Open questions (for discussion — no answers)
What is a diploma proof of — now?
Ban the tool — or require it?
What do we grade when output is free?
If tutoring is finally affordable — why do we still lecture?
